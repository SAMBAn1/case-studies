CASE STUDY 02

PRODUCT VISION & WORKING DEMO

Two worklists.
Two different failures.
One hybrid model.

A rule-based worklist and a data-driven worklist solved different halves of the same problem — and each broke the half the other one fixed. Here's the operating model that keeps both.

scroll↓

THE PROBLEM

Who gets called first is the only question that matters.

Every collections team opens one screen each morning and asks the same question: who do I work first? Get that answer right and cash comes in faster, disputes get caught while they're still small, and nobody wastes a call on an account that already paid. Get it wrong — and it goes wrong constantly — and collectors either drown in a list they've stopped trusting, or lose the specific judgment calls their business was actually built on.

Two different products tried to answer that question, and each one solved exactly half of it.

One handed collectors total control: segments, rules, named ownership, and actions specific enough to mirror precisely how the business runs — "send this letter when an invoice is 15 days past due" could be modeled exactly as written. It worked, until the rulebook grew so large that changing anything became a consulting engagement nobody wanted to open.

The other handed the decision to a model: data-driven scoring, a cleaner setup, live in days instead of months. But nobody could explain why a customer ranked where it did, actions collapsed into a generic "Call," and ownership could shift under a collector without warning.

Neither team was wrong. Both shipped half an answer. Below is exactly where each one broke — and what the other half looks like.

RULE-BASED MODEL

Granular control, expensive to run

  • Segment → strategy → rule mapping that mirrors real SOPs
  • Named, predictable assignment
  • Specific, auditable actions
  • Rules proliferate until nobody can maintain the strategy tree
  • Every change is a consulting engagement
  • Prioritization quality is weak — rules don't rank, they just fire
VS

DATA-DRIVEN MODEL

Smart scoring, opaque and rigid

  • Normalized, data-driven scoring out of the box
  • Correspondence cleanly decoupled from the worklist
  • Fast initial setup, no strategy tree to design
  • Black-box prioritization — nobody can explain a rank
  • Actions are generic: "Call" is not an instruction
  • Dynamic assignment breaks ownership continuity

THE VISION

Four questions. Four pillars. Independently answered, never entangled.

The fix isn't picking a side. It's refusing to let one mechanism decide priority, ownership, action, and visibility all at once — the exact entanglement that made both models fragile.

"Who should matter more?"

Prioritization

Data-driven scoring, group-level ranking, and priority bands — with every score fully explainable, never a black box.

"Who owns this customer?"

Allocation

Fixed rules, uniform distribution, or shared pool — ownership stays stable. Bandwidth shapes focus, never silently reassigns.

"What should be done?"

Action

Specific business instructions, not generic labels — with sequencing, expiry, and completion criteria a collector can trust.

"What shows up today, and why?"

Worklist

Today's Focus and Eligible Backlog, with eligibility, cooldown, and every inclusion or suppression fully explained.
